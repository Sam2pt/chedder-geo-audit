If you read one post on this blog about high-leverage AI search wins, make it this one. A Wikipedia entry for your brand is one of the most disproportionately powerful things you can have. AI engines weight Wikipedia citations heavily because the platform is well-edited, well-linked, and structured in ways large language models love.

Most CPG brands either don't have a Wikipedia entry or have a thin one nobody maintains. Both states are fixable. Neither is fixable in the way founders typically try to fix them.

## The thing you can't do (and why brands keep getting reverted)

The reflex when a founder learns about Wikipedia's value is to write a draft, publish it under their own account, and call it done. This fails almost every time. The page either gets flagged as promotional, marked for deletion, or gets reverted to a stub by editors within 24 hours.

Wikipedia's editing community has a specific (and reasonable) suspicion of brand-self-authorship. They've been burned by enough thinly-veiled marketing copy that the bar for any new commercial entry is high, and the bar for self-authored commercial entries is higher still. The site has explicit guidelines (WP:NOTPROMOTION, WP:COI) that get cited the moment a brand-named account creates a brand-named page.

So the goal isn't to write your own page. The goal is to BE WORTH writing about, and then to make it easy for someone else to write the page.

## The notability bar (and how to check if you cross it)

Wikipedia has a published standard called "notability" that determines whether a topic qualifies for an entry. For a commercial brand, the practical interpretation is:

You need at least 3-5 independent, reliable sources that have written about your brand in substantive depth. "Substantive" means a paragraph or more of actual content, not a press release mention. "Reliable" means publishers with editorial standards — major newspapers, trade publications, recognized industry magazines. "Independent" means not authored by you, your PR firm, or your team.

What COUNTS as a reliable source:

- New York Times, Wall Street Journal, BBC, Reuters
- Trade pubs like AdAge, RetailDive, Modern Retail, Glossy, Vogue Business
- Reputable industry blogs that have been around 5+ years
- Books, academic papers (rare for CPG, but valuable when they exist)

What does NOT count:

- Press releases on PRNewswire or Business Wire
- Your own blog posts
- Sponsored content (even from major publishers)
- Listicles that briefly mention you alongside 30 other brands
- TechCrunch funding announcements (these are seen as PR-driven; some editors push back)

If you can list 3-5 sources from the "counts" column that have written about your brand specifically, you cross the notability bar.

## What to do once you cross it

This is the part most guides skip. Even with sources in hand, you still need to navigate the cultural norms of Wikipedia.

**Option 1: hire a Wikipedia editor (the right kind)**

There are professional Wikipedia editors who specialize in writing entries for notable brands while staying within the community's guidelines. They write in neutral voice, cite real sources, disclose their paid relationship to the brand (which is required), and submit through Articles for Creation.

A reputable Wikipedia editor for a notable brand will charge $1,500-$5,000 for a properly-written entry. They will NOT guarantee approval — no one can — but their approval rate is usually 70-85% versus the ~10% rate of unsupported brand attempts.

Search for "Wikipedia editor for hire" and look for editors who:

- Disclose every paid edit publicly (it's a community requirement)
- Have a clean public edit history with no recent blocks
- Show example entries they've gotten through Articles for Creation

Avoid anyone who promises guaranteed approval or claims they have "connections."

**Option 2: pitch a relevant editor without paying**

Find a Wikipedia editor who actively edits in your category (mattresses, beauty, food, whatever) and pitch them on writing an entry. This is harder but free.

Find editors by:

- Going to a Wikipedia page in your category (e.g., "Bed-in-a-box")
- Click "View history" 
- Look for usernames that have made multiple substantive edits over time
- Click their username, then their "User talk" page
- Politely ask if they'd be interested in evaluating your brand for notability and writing an entry if it qualifies

Don't pay them. Don't promise them anything. Don't bombard. One polite, well-researched pitch with your 3-5 source list, then move on if they're not interested.

**Option 3: start with Wikidata**

Wikidata is Wikipedia's structured-data sibling. It has a much lower notability bar and any properly-cited entity can be added. It also feeds heavily into AI engine training data.

Create your Wikidata entry first. It's a simpler, less-policed environment. Once your Wikidata entry exists with proper citations, the path to a Wikipedia entry becomes easier (you have a structured starting point).

Wikidata entries take 30 minutes to set up properly. They're free.

## What gets you reverted, every time

If you try any of these, expect immediate reversion:

- Creating an account in your brand's name and writing the entry
- Hiring someone who doesn't disclose the paid relationship
- Writing in promotional tone ("Casper is the leading...")
- Citing only your own website as a source
- Adding the entry, then immediately editing it to add more about your brand
- Mass-creating entries for your product line (one entry for the brand is normal; one entry per SKU is not)
- Removing critical content other editors add

## The patience this requires

A real Wikipedia entry for your brand, properly written, takes 4-8 weeks from the first move to a stable published page. The community process is slow on purpose — speed is a yellow flag for promotional editing.

Don't push. Don't message editors twice. Don't argue if your draft gets rejected; rewrite to address the feedback.

A clean, well-cited Wikipedia entry for your brand becomes a permanent fixture in how AI engines describe you. We've seen brands move from "AI engine doesn't know who we are" to "AI engine names us in 30% of category prompts" in the 6 months following a Wikipedia entry going live.
